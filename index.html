SHIGERU BAN

Innovative Japanese architect and Pritzker winner

Shigeru Ban standing inside one of his buildings

Life and work of Shigeru Ban

  • 1957 - Born in Tokyo, Japan

  • 1977/1984 - Travels to EEUU to study architecture.

  • 1985 - Opened up his practice in Tokyo, Over the next year, he was commissioned for the organization and designed of several projects. In addition to this, Ban also began to design a series of houses as case studies

  • 1994 - Ban proposed a paper-tube shelters design to the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees as a possible solution to the influx caused by the two million refugees who had escaped the Rwandan Civil War

  • 1995 - Following the Kobe Earthquake Shigeru built the “Paper Log House” to accommodate the Vietnamese refugees, and later, he built the Takatori “Paper Church” along with student volunteers. He also founded NGO Voluntary Architects Network in order to provide disaster relief assistance.

  • 2000 - Collaborated with German architect and engineer, Frei Otto, and constructed a gigantic paper-tube grid shell structure for Hanover Expo's Japan Pavilion in Germany. This design won world-wide acclaim for its recyclable architecture.

  • 2001 - Appointed as a Professor of Environment and Information Studies at Keio University

  • 2010 - Served at Harvard and Cornell Universities as a visiting professor.

  • 2011 - Appointed as a Professor at Kyoto University of Art and Design

  • 2014 - Won the Pritzker award for responding with creativity and high quality design to extreme situations caused by devastating natural disasters. For his buildings that provide shelter, community centers, and spiritual places for those who have suffered tremendous loss and destruction.
